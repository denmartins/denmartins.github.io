Short Bio

I am a PostDoc researcher at the Machine Learning and Data Engineering group of the University of Münster, Germany. I finished my Ph.D. in January 2020, at University of Münster, under the supervision of Prof. Dr. Gottfried Vossen (Databases and Information Systems group) and co-supervision of Prof. Dr. Fernando Buarque de Lima Neto (Computational Intelligence Research Group). My research interests focus on the application of Computational Intelligence and Machine Learning in Data Management for enabling non-technical users to query and purchase data. In 2014, I received my Master's degree in Computer Engineering from the University of Pernambuco, Brazil, where I researched Computational Semiotics approaches for intelligent decision-making support.

Research

Query Reverse Engineering from Examples

Formulating database queries in terms of SQL is often a challenge for journalists, business administrators, and the growing number of non-database experts that need to access and explore data. Query Reverse Engineering (QRE) from examples can help users with limited technical expertise to formulate database queries without requiring them to be database nor programming experts knowledge. In QRE, users are asked to provide a set of data examples that satisfy their information needs and the system tries to (semi-)automatically construct an accurate query, if one exists.

Supporting Data Purchase in Data Marketplaces

The need for high-quality data has motivated the development of data marketplaces, i.e., online platforms that host data of various domains and serve as a central access point for people and organizations that aim to buy and sell data. Nevertheless, data marketplaces offer almost no help for non-technical data consumers who frequently have only limited data-specific knowledge and present a vague idea about the characteristics of data that fulfills needs.

Supporting Efficient Decision-Making via Computational Intelligence and Semiotics

Large, complex, and semi-structured decision problems challenge both organizations and society. These problems often present a large set of influencing factors that cannot be manually nor thoroughly analyzed due to the limitations of human cognitive capacity or the lack of a structured problem-solving methodology. Fortunately, as data becomes more available, Computational Semiotics and Computational Intelligence techniques are employed to combine collective and expert knowledge to support complex decision-making such that perceptual, personal, and contextual signals can be efficiently exploited.
